# Anti-Corruption Court Plan

**Created:** 2026-04-17
**Status:** Draft

---

## Overview

A citizen-initiated court focused on public corruption cases. The court is financed by the
claimants themselves via a signature-weighted filing fee, held in escrow until the case resolves.
Verdicts are rendered by a 15-member jury drawn from a standing pool of at least 1,000
qualified judges, with an 80% supermajority required to convict.

The design pillars below define what counts as corruption in this court's scope, how filings are
initiated, how money is custodied, how the double-jeopardy boundary is drawn, how verdicts are
rendered, and why serving on the jury should be treated as a prestige appointment in a legal
career.

---

## 0. What Counts as Corruption

### Working definition

> **Corruption** is the use of entrusted power — public, corporate, or institutional — for
> private benefit, in a way that diffusely harms a population that has no practical standing to
> sue individually.

Three features matter:

1. **Entrusted power.** The actor holds a role (elected office, regulator, fiduciary, executive,
   board seat, licensed professional) whose authority exists to serve someone other than
   themselves.
2. **Private benefit.** Money, political capital, career advancement, favors, access, or
   preservation of status — not necessarily a suitcase of cash.
3. **Diffuse harm.** The cost is spread across a population (taxpayers, ratepayers, consumers,
   a city's residents, a profession's clients, the voting public), so no single victim has
   enough stake to prosecute alone, and conventional criminal courts struggle to assign
   specific-victim standing.

This last feature is the core reason this court exists. **The public is the victim, but the
public is not a plaintiff in any existing venue.** Conventional criminal law requires a specific
victim with specific damages; conventional civil law requires standing and individualized harm.
Corruption slips between the two.

### Criminal vs. blatant-but-legal

This court explicitly covers conduct that is **blatant, harmful, and self-dealing but not
clearly criminal under existing statute**. Many of the most damaging corruption patterns in
modern society are structured precisely to stay on the technical side of the law:

- Passing a regulation that benefits a specific donor class, worded generally enough that no
  quid-pro-quo is provable.
- Revolving-door employment where the "reward" for regulatory favorable treatment arrives two
  years later as a board seat.
- Pension fund investment decisions that benefit the decider's social network while producing
  mediocre returns for beneficiaries.
- Corporate board entrenchment — CEO pay and governance choices that transfer value from
  diffuse shareholders to a small executive group.
- Procurement that is technically compliant but consistently routed to connected vendors.
- Academic or scientific capture where industry-funded researchers shape findings that drive
  public policy.

A case filed here does not need to allege a crime. It needs to allege:

- Entrusted power,
- Self-interested use of that power,
- Diffuse public harm,
- Evidence that the conduct is **blatant** — obvious enough that reasonable observers agree
  it's out of bounds, even if the relevant statute was carefully sidestepped.

### Retribution model

Because much of the conduct in scope isn't criminal, this court cannot impose prison sentences.
Its available remedies are:

- **Monetary damages** paid by the defendant into a remediation fund and/or pro-rata to the
  signer/sponsors as partial standing for the public.
- **Disgorgement** of the private benefit (fees, stock, pension boosts, contract margins).
- **Public findings** — a formal, bar-association-recognized finding of corruption, attached to
  the defendant's professional record.
- **Bar / license referrals** — where the defendant holds a professional license (attorney,
  CPA, medical, financial advisor), the finding is referred to the licensing body.
- **Ineligibility for future entrusted roles** — a binding commitment from participating
  institutions (subscribing employers, pension funds, boards) not to place convicted
  individuals in fiduciary roles for a defined period. This is opt-in for institutions but
  becomes a meaningful consequence once the subscriber list is large.

### Case-study catalog

The court maintains a **living catalog of archetype cases** to calibrate the jury pool and the
intake reviewers on what does and doesn't meet the bar. Starter archetypes:

- **Regulatory capture.** A regulator issues guidance that aligns suspiciously well with the
  commercial interests of a former or future employer. Harm is diffuse across a whole industry's
  customers.
- **Pension self-dealing.** Trustees of a public pension allocate to a fund run by a college
  classmate, producing mediocre returns and high fees. Harm is diffuse across current and
  future retirees.
- **Municipal procurement steering.** A city repeatedly awards contracts to a narrow set of
  vendors, always compliant on paper, always above market. Harm is diffuse across taxpayers.
- **Sham-donation influence.** A public official receives repeated "speaking fees" or
  foundation donations from entities with matters before them. Each individual payment is
  legal; the pattern is obvious.
- **Disclosure laundering.** An executive arranges compensation so that it technically complies
  with disclosure rules but materially misleads shareholders about alignment of incentives.
- **Executive entrenchment.** A board, populated through a circular network of reciprocal
  directorships, awards pay and protections that transfer value from diffuse shareholders to
  the CEO and themselves.
- **Academic capture.** Industry-funded research is used to shape policy in ways that
  predictably benefit the funder and harm the public interest the research purported to serve.
- **Revolving-door reward.** A regulator takes regulator-friendly positions during tenure, then
  joins the regulated entity at above-market compensation within a short window.
- **Insider dealing by public servants.** A government employee, legislator, regulator, staffer,
  judge, central banker, or agency contractor trades (personally, through a spouse, or through
  a blind-in-name-only vehicle) on information they obtained because of their role —
  non-public knowledge of upcoming legislation, regulatory actions, contract awards, rate
  decisions, enforcement actions, investigations, or market-moving briefings. The harm is
  classically diffuse: the general public was not given a fair shot at the same information,
  and the official's private gain came directly out of the broader market's pocket.

  Public-sector insider dealing is a first-class target of this court for three reasons:
  1. Securities-law insider-trading doctrine was built around corporate fiduciaries and often
     struggles to reach legislators, staffers, and executive-branch personnel — existing
     statutes like the STOCK Act rely on disclosure rather than prohibition, and enforcement
     is thin.
  2. The information asymmetry is total: the official literally set the event in motion.
  3. The harm is textbook diffuse — every retail participant on the other side of those
     trades, and every citizen whose trust in public institutions erodes.

  Scope includes but is not limited to: stock and option trades, real-estate purchases ahead of
  zoning or infrastructure decisions, commodity positioning ahead of agricultural or energy
  policy, crypto positioning ahead of regulatory announcements, and tipping family members or
  donors who trade on the tip.

- **Patent-system abuse.** Use of a government-granted monopoly — the patent — to extract rent
  rather than to reward invention. The patent system is entrusted power by design: the public
  grants a time-limited monopoly in exchange for disclosure of a genuine invention, on the
  bargain that the public gets the invention in the public domain once the term expires. Many of
  the most lucrative modern patent strategies are structured to evade that bargain while staying
  technically compliant.

  Recurring patterns:
  - **Evergreening.** Cascading minor-variation patents (new formulation, new crystalline form,
    new dosing regimen) to extend a monopoly past its intended term. Most visible in
    pharmaceuticals, where a single blockbuster drug accumulates 50+ patents that delay generic
    entry by years.
  - **Pay-for-delay settlements.** Brand-name patent-holders pay generic manufacturers to *not*
    challenge the patent and *not* launch a generic. The brand preserves monopoly pricing; the
    generic collects a settlement larger than its expected launch profit; consumers pay the
    spread for years. The FTC has pursued these since *FTC v. Actavis* (2013) but reverse-payment
    arrangements remain widespread under increasingly indirect structures.
  - **Patent-troll (non-practicing-entity) litigation.** Entities that produce nothing buy
    portfolios and sue operating companies for settlements calibrated to be smaller than defense
    cost. Social value transferred is zero; private rent is pure extraction. Venue-shopping
    (historically E.D. Tex.) compounds the imbalance.
  - **Patent thickets and cross-licensing cartels.** Dense overlapping patents held across a
    small group of incumbents — each licenses to each other, raising the effective cost of
    entry for anyone outside the group. No single patent is decisive; the aggregate is a moat.
  - **Submarine patents.** Prosecution strategies that delay patent issuance until an industry
    has standardized on a technology, then surface to demand licensing from everyone now
    locked in.
  - **Standards-essential-patent (SEP) holdup.** A patent-holder contributes a patent to a
    technical standard under a FRAND commitment (fair, reasonable, and non-discriminatory
    licensing), then extracts supra-FRAND royalties once the standard has been widely adopted
    and implementers cannot realistically design around it.
  - **Capture of publicly-funded research.** Under Bayh-Dole and adjacent frameworks,
    universities and government-funded labs patent taxpayer-funded research and license it
    exclusively to a single firm, converting public R&D into a private monopoly with no
    corresponding return to the funding public.
  - **USPTO examiner capture.** Examiner workload targets, promotion structures, and
    post-employment pipelines into the patent bar produce systemic under-examination of
    borderline applications. Weak examination is itself a transfer from diffuse public to
    concentrated patent-holder.

  Patent-system abuse is a first-class target of this court for three reasons:
  1. **Standing is classically diffuse.** Individual consumers of a single overpriced drug have
     no practical standing to challenge an evergreening strategy. Downstream innovators crushed
     by a patent thicket cannot sue for the product they never got to attempt. The public whose
     tax dollars funded captured research has no plaintiff.
  2. **Existing law reaches only the most extreme cases.** Antitrust reaches pay-for-delay after
     *Actavis* but not thicket construction or the full evergreening pattern. Patent law itself
     is the thing being abused; asking the patent system to discipline patent strategy is
     circular. *Alice v. CLS Bank* and *TC Heartland* helped at the margins but most of the
     rent-extraction stack remains legal.
  3. **The aggregate harm is enormous.** US drug spending above unimpeded-generic baseline,
     the technology-sector licensing tax, and the opportunity cost of suppressed innovation add
     up to hundreds of billions annually, while the beneficiaries are a small and identifiable
     class.

  Scope includes but is not limited to: pharmaceutical evergreening and reverse-payment
  settlement patterns, NPE litigation campaigns above a volume threshold, SEP/FRAND holdup,
  Bayh-Dole exclusive licensing of taxpayer-funded research under terms inconsistent with
  reasonable public return, and coordinated thicket maintenance by industry incumbents where
  cross-licensing patterns exclude specific entrants. Evidence is often auditable from public
  records: patent filings, licensing deals disclosed in SEC filings or in litigation, settlement
  terms in paragraph-IV cases, USPTO prosecution histories, and examiner-to-firm employment
  timelines. Proportionate remedies range from disgorgement of excess monopoly rents and bar
  referrals (for the patent attorneys involved) through to public findings that feed PTAB
  inter-partes review and legislative reform pressure.

Each case study is documented with: the pattern, why existing law struggles to reach it, what
evidence would be decisive, and what remedy would be proportionate. The catalog is public, both
as a deterrent and as guidance for prospective plaintiffs.

## 1. Plaintiff-Chosen Filing Tier (Sliding Scale)

When filing online, the **initial plaintiff chooses the signature tier** they want to target
before the filing opens for signing. The chosen tier determines how many verified signatures are
required, the per-signer/sponsor fee, the total escrow pot, and what stage of court work the filing
unlocks.

Lower tiers are cheaper per signer/sponsor but produce smaller pots and unlock only preliminary stages;
higher tiers concentrate more money behind a filing and unlock the full trial. The plaintiff
picks based on how much public support they believe they can marshal and how far they want the
case to proceed on first filing.

### Tier schedule

| Tier     | Signer/Sponsors | Per signer/sponsor | Total pot | What it unlocks                                |
| -------- | ------- | ---------- | --------- | ---------------------------------------------- |
| Screen   | 100     | $30        | $3,000    | Complaint screening + written recommendation   |
| Inquiry  | 500     | $20        | $10,000   | Preliminary investigation + expert evaluation  |
| Trial-1  | 1,000   | $30        | $30,000   | Full trial (minimum viable budget)             |
| Trial-2  | 2,000   | $15        | $30,000   | Full trial                                     |
| Trial-3  | 3,000   | $10        | $30,000   | Full trial                                     |
| Trial-5  | 5,000   | $6         | $30,000   | Full trial + appeal reserve                    |
| Trial-10 | 10,000  | $3         | $30,000   | Full trial + max public-interest signal        |

### Sliding-scale formula (trial tiers)

For the trial tiers (1,000–10,000 signer/sponsors), per-signer/sponsor cost is derived from a fixed pot:

```
cost_per_sponsor = $30,000 / signature_count
```

This is the floor cost of taking a case through a real jury trial through first review. Trial
tiers above 10,000 signer/sponsors stay at $3 per signer/sponsor, with overages going into a case reserve for
discovery, expert fees, and appeal defense rather than shrinking skin in the game toward zero.

### Graduation

Sub-trial tiers (Screen, Inquiry) do not end a matter — if the findings warrant it, signer/sponsors are
offered the chance to **graduate** the filing to the next tier by topping up their pledge, or to
re-open signature collection at the higher tier. Graduation preserves the same defendant/conduct/
time-window triple (see Section 3), so work product carries over instead of being redone.

### Rules

- **Tier selection is binding at filing open.** The plaintiff cannot change tiers after signing
  has begun; they must close and re-file (without charging existing signer/sponsors) to move tiers.
- **Verification:** each signer/sponsor must be a natural person with verified identity (KYC-light),
  one signature per person per filing. Duplicate detection runs before the escrow hold is
  placed.
- **Commitment timing:** signer/sponsors pledge at signing; the payment is only captured once the
  tier's signature count is reached AND the filing is accepted for review. This prevents
  signer/sponsors from being charged for filings that never proceed.
- **Minimum viability:** a filing that does not reach its chosen tier's signature count within
  the collection window is cancelled and no charges settle.

## 2. Escrow & Banking Infrastructure

All filing fees are pooled into a per-case escrow account. No money touches the court operator's
general ledger until it is released by a defined trigger.

### Custodian options

- **Stripe Treasury / Stripe Connect custodial accounts** — fast to integrate, per-case sub-
  ledgers, strong API.
- **Column / Evolve / Mercury Treasury** — true bank partners with segregated custodial
  accounting, better for regulator optics.
- **State-chartered trust company** — strongest story for long-running cases and public trust,
  slower to onboard.

Default choice for v1: Stripe Treasury sub-accounts with a partner bank, with a migration path
to a chartered trust company once case volume justifies the overhead.

### Flow of funds

1. **Signer/Sponsor pledge** — card auth / ACH debit authorization captured, not charged.
2. **Filing accepted** — charges captured in batch; funds settle into the case escrow.
3. **Case resolves** — escrow is disbursed per the verdict rule below.

### Disbursement rules

- **Conviction with damages awarded:**
  1. Court operating costs are reimbursed from the convicted party's damages, not from the
     escrow pool.
  2. Claimant escrow is refunded pro-rata, plus any damage award share owed to them.
  3. Any surplus stays in the case reserve for appeal defense.
- **Conviction with no monetary damages:** claimants receive a partial refund (defined percentage
  of remaining escrow after trial costs).
- **Acquittal:** escrow covers trial costs; residual refunded pro-rata to signer/sponsors. Signer/Sponsors are
  warned of this risk at pledge time.

### Tax treatment

The court operator is structured as a **501(c)(3) or 501(c)(4)** civic-legal foundation so that:

- Non-refunded portions of signer/sponsor contributions are deductible as charitable contributions to a
  public-interest legal fund.
- Refunded portions are not deductible (they never left the signer/sponsor's tax basis).
- Year-end, each signer/sponsor receives a consolidated statement showing the deductible amount across
  all filings they supported.

A tax opinion letter is required before v1 launch to confirm treatment — this determines whether
501(c)(3) (deductible, apolitical) or 501(c)(4) (non-deductible, permits advocacy) is the right
structure.

## 3. Double Jeopardy Without Lifetime Immunity

The court must prevent re-litigation of the same conduct, without granting blanket immunity for
unrelated future conduct by the same person.

### Scoping rule: an infraction is a `(defendant, conduct, time window)` triple

Every filing is indexed on:

- **Defendant** — specific natural or legal person.
- **Conduct** — a specific act or pattern of acts (e.g., "accepting undisclosed payment from
  Vendor X in exchange for contract award Y"), described with enough specificity that a reviewer
  can judge whether a future filing is the same conduct or a different one.
- **Time window** — the dates during which the conduct occurred.

A conviction or acquittal binds only that triple. The same defendant may be tried again for:

- **Different conduct** within the same time window (e.g., a different bribe, a different leak).
- **The same conduct type** in a different time window (e.g., a later bribe from a different
  vendor).
- **New facts** that did not exist and could not reasonably have been known at the original
  trial (narrow carve-out for fraud-on-the-court).

### Enforcement mechanism

- **Filing intake** computes a conduct fingerprint (structured fields + embedding of the factual
  narrative) and searches prior filings against the same defendant.
- **Collision review:** if the embedding + structured match crosses a similarity threshold, a
  three-judge panel reviews before the filing is allowed to proceed. Decisions are appealable.
- **Public docket:** every filing, verdict, and the associated triple is published so signer/sponsors
  and defendants both know the scope of what has already been adjudicated.

## 4. 15-Juror Panel, 80% Supermajority, Multi-Defendant Verdicts

### The judge pool

A core design principle of the court is a **large, standing pool of qualified judges — at least
1,000** sitting at any time. Case assignments are drawn randomly from this pool, and
**different judges are assigned to different stages of the same case**, so that the reviewer who
screens the filing is not the investigator, is not the pretrial motions judge, is not on the
15-person trial panel, and is not on any appeal panel.

This per-stage rotation is the court's single strongest anti-bias mechanism:

- No judge carries a prior impression of the defendant or the claimant pool from an earlier
  stage into the stage they're currently deciding.
- No small cadre of judges can be lobbied, flattered, or threatened into shaping a case end-to-
  end.
- A plaintiff or defendant cannot shop for (or against) a specific judge, because the pool is
  large and the assignment is random and staged.
- A judge's total exposure to any one case is bounded, which keeps the case load sustainable
  across the pool.

The pool must be large enough (≥1,000) that the birthday-problem odds of the same judge
recurring across stages of the same case are vanishingly small, and that conflict-of-interest
strikes leave plenty of eligible candidates for every stage.

### Composition

- **15 jurors** per trial, randomly drawn from the judge pool (filtered to those who have not
  previously served on any stage of this case).
- **Random selection** runs through a publicly verifiable lottery (commit-reveal on a public
  ledger) from the eligible pool, with strikes for conflicts of interest.
- Jurors are sequestered only for deliberation, not the full trial.

### Conviction threshold

- **12 of 15 = 80%** required to convict on any single charge.
- **Non-unanimous** by design — one or two holdouts cannot block a conviction the supermajority
  believes is sound, but the 80% bar keeps the standard well above a simple majority.
- **Acquittal** requires 4 or more not-guilty votes (the inverse threshold).
- **Hung panel** (neither threshold met) → retrial permitted with a fresh panel; this does not
  trigger double jeopardy because no verdict was rendered.

### Multi-defendant cases

- A single filing may name multiple defendants who are alleged to have acted in concert.
- The panel votes **independently per defendant per charge**. Each (defendant, charge) pair is
  its own 12-of-15 question.
- This lets the jury convict the principals while acquitting peripheral actors in one trial,
  rather than forcing a messy package-deal verdict.

### Vote transparency

- Individual juror votes are sealed for a fixed cooling-off period (e.g., 10 years) to prevent
  intimidation, then released with the public docket for historical accountability.

## 5. Prestige of Service

Service on this court should be positioned as one of the high-status appointments in a legal
career — comparable to a federal clerkship or a term on a state bar disciplinary board — so that
the juror pool self-selects for seriousness rather than availability.

### Eligibility

- Licensed attorney, retired judge, or law professor with a minimum number of years in practice.
- Good standing with the relevant bar.
- No active political candidacy or lobbyist registration during service.
- The pool is sized to **at least 1,000 active judges** (see Section 4) so that stage rotation
  and conflict strikes always leave a healthy draw of eligible candidates.

### Term and compensation

- **Fixed term** (e.g., 3 years) with a cap on cases served, to prevent capture and to keep the
  pool refreshing.
- **Stipend** per case, set at a level that signals seriousness without creating a financial
  incentive to convict or acquit. The stipend is the same regardless of verdict.

### Credentialing

- **Official commission** on appointment — a credential that appears on résumés and bar
  profiles, analogous to "Commissioner, State Ethics Board."
- **CLE credit** (continuing legal education) granted for service, recognized by participating
  bar associations.
- **Alumni network** of former jurors — a durable professional community that outlasts any
  individual case.

### Signaling

- High-profile appointment announcements (press release per class of jurors seated).
- Named opinions: for cases that set precedent, the lead juror's reasoning is published under
  their name (with the rest of the panel's votes tallied but anonymous until the sealing period
  ends).
- Partnership-track firms are encouraged (via a published list) to treat service as billable
  pro-bono time rather than vacation — this is the lever that makes the appointment actually
  available to mid-career practitioners.

### Anti-capture safeguards

- **Random selection** inside the qualified pool means no one campaigns their way onto a
  specific case.
- **Conflict disclosures** required before every case; strikes are public.
- **Term limits** keep any single juror from being seated on a disproportionate number of cases.

## Historical Precedents (Last ~100 Years)

No prior country has run exactly this design — **citizen-initiated, signature-funded, escrow-
backed, with a standing 1,000+ judge pool and per-stage random rotation**. But there are many
adjacent attempts at dedicated anti-corruption institutions, and their track records teach
specific lessons that shape this plan.

### Cases that largely functioned

- **Hong Kong ICAC (1974–present).** Set up after systemic police corruption in the early
  1970s. Fully independent of the government it polices, with three divisions: enforcement,
  prevention, education. Widely credited with moving HK from deeply corrupt to one of the
  cleanest jurisdictions in Asia within a generation. **Lesson: institutional independence and
  long time horizon are the two decisive inputs.**
- **Singapore CPIB (1952–present).** Reports directly to the Prime Minister rather than a
  ministry. Small, elite, high-status service. Effective partly because of political backing,
  which is also its vulnerability — it depends on the PM's continuing support. **Lesson:
  political backing works until it doesn't; structural independence is more durable.**
- **Indonesia KPK (2002–2019 as originally designed).** A dedicated anti-corruption court
  (Tipikor) with conviction rates near 100% in its early years, including ministers, MPs, and
  governors. Systematically weakened by 2019 legislation that reduced its independence after it
  got too close to the political class. **Lesson: effective anti-corruption bodies get
  attacked, and the attack typically comes through "reform" legislation — structural defenses
  against defunding and scope narrowing matter as much as the design itself.**
- **South Korea high-official prosecutions.** A series of special prosecutors and, more
  recently, the Corruption Investigation Office for High-Ranking Officials (2021), have
  convicted multiple former presidents. **Lesson: cultural and judicial willingness to
  convict sitting/former heads of state is learnable — successful high-profile convictions
  raise the ceiling for future cases.**
- **Philippines Sandiganbayan (1979–present).** Constitutional-basis special graft court for
  public officials. Convicted former President Estrada. Uneven effectiveness across decades
  but institutionally durable. **Lesson: constitutional entrenchment survives political
  weather better than statutory creation.**

### Cases that partially functioned, then were captured or dismantled

- **Guatemala CICIG (2007–2019).** UN-backed hybrid commission. Highly effective —
  investigations brought down President Pérez Molina in 2015. When it then turned toward
  President Morales, he simply did not renew its mandate and expelled it. **Lesson: a body
  dependent on political mandate-renewal will be cut off the moment it becomes effective
  enough to threaten the mandate-renewer. The proposed court's citizen-funding model is a
  direct response to this failure mode.**
- **South Africa Scorpions (DSO, 2001–2009).** Effective elite anti-graft unit; prosecuted a
  deputy president. Disbanded by the ruling party when inconvenient, replaced by the weaker
  "Hawks" inside the police. **Lesson: same pattern as Guatemala — effectiveness triggers
  dismantling unless the body's existence is outside the majority's reach.**
- **Brazil Operation Car Wash / Lava Jato (2014–2021).** Convicted a former president and
  many executives. Later discredited by leaks showing coordination between the lead judge
  (Sergio Moro) and prosecutors, which undermined prior convictions and the body's legitimacy.
  **Lesson: this is exactly the bias scenario the per-stage judge rotation in Section 4 is
  designed to prevent. When the same judge shepherds a case end-to-end, the combination of
  commitment and ego produces coordination with prosecutors that is nearly impossible to
  detect from outside, and nearly impossible to recover from once exposed.**
- **Italy Mani Pulite (1992–mid-1990s).** Milan prosecutors dismantled the Christian Democrat
  / Socialist power structure. Succeeded in toppling the old corrupt system, failed to
  prevent a new corrupt system (Berlusconi-era) from replacing it. **Lesson: prosecutions
  without institutional reform are a one-shot cleansing, not a durable equilibrium. Structural
  features — not heroic prosecutors — are what last.**
- **Romania DNA (2002–present).** Convicted many high-level officials under Laura Codruța
  Kövesi. She was fired in 2018 via government maneuver; the DNA has been repeatedly targeted
  by legal "reforms" since. **Lesson: personalization of anti-corruption success (one
  celebrated prosecutor) creates a single point of attack — large pools of rotated
  decision-makers are harder to neutralize than individual stars.**
- **Ukraine NABU / HACC (2015, 2019).** Post-Maidan reforms, partly conditioned on by the IMF
  and EU. HACC is a dedicated anti-corruption court. Some successes, persistent political
  pressure, ongoing war complicates evaluation. **Lesson: external conditionality (IMF, EU)
  can force creation of an independent body that domestic politics would never produce on its
  own — but external conditionality is not a sustainable long-term defense.**

### Cases that largely failed

- **US Independent Counsel statute (1978–1999).** Post-Watergate creation allowing
  independent investigation of the executive branch. Allowed to expire after the Starr
  investigation of Clinton, which was widely perceived as partisan and without proportion.
  Replaced by a weaker regulation-based "special counsel" role inside DOJ. **Lesson: scope
  control matters. An investigator with unlimited runway and unlimited scope will drift into
  fishing expeditions, burn legitimacy, and cause the body itself to be dismantled. The
  proposed court's defendant/conduct/time-window scoping in Section 3 is designed around this
  failure.**
- **Pakistan NAB (1999–present).** Widely perceived as weaponized against political
  opponents of whichever government holds power. **Lesson: an anti-corruption body that
  answers to the executive becomes a political tool. Random juror draw from a 1,000-judge pool
  is the structural answer to this — no one party can populate it.**
- **Most national-level anti-corruption agencies in sub-Saharan Africa and Latin America.**
  Generally under-resourced, politically dependent, and institutionally weak; often a
  performance of reform for foreign donors rather than a functional check. **Lesson:
  anti-corruption infrastructure without independent funding is theater.**

### What has never been tried

No major jurisdiction in the last century has combined, in one body:

- **Citizen-initiated filings** with no gatekeeper inside the government being policed,
- **Citizen-funded escrow** as the primary revenue source (rather than government budget or
  international donors),
- **A 1,000+ standing judge pool** with per-stage random rotation explicitly designed against
  the Lava-Jato-style end-to-end shepherding bias,
- **A supermajority (not unanimous, not simple majority) verdict rule** sized to be robust to
  one or two captured jurors,
- **An explicit scope on diffuse-harm conduct** that may be blatant but not criminal.

Each of these features is a direct response to a documented failure mode above. The design is
not speculative in its parts — every part has a historical case attached — but the combination
is novel, and the whitepaper should present it as such.

### Synthesized lessons driving this design

1. **Independence from the policed entity is decisive.** (ICAC, KPK before 2019 vs. CICIG,
   Scorpions.)
2. **Effectiveness triggers attack.** Assume the body will be attacked the moment it matters.
   Build structural defenses (citizen funding, constitutional entrenchment, distributed
   decision-making) rather than relying on political goodwill.
3. **End-to-end shepherding of a case by one judge / prosecutor is a bias and legitimacy trap.**
   (Lava Jato.) Rotate by stage across a large pool.
4. **Scope discipline prevents fishing expeditions.** (US Independent Counsel.) The
   defendant/conduct/time-window triple is the answer.
5. **Personalization creates a single point of attack.** (Romania DNA.) A pool of 1,000 is
   harder to decapitate than one celebrated prosecutor.
6. **Prosecutions alone are a one-shot cleansing, not a durable equilibrium.** (Mani Pulite.)
   Structural features, not heroics, are what persist.
